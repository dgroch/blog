I used to think writing was something you did *after* you'd figured things out. A packaging step. You think, then you write.

I had it backwards.

Writing is the thinking. The act of putting words in sequence — of forcing vague intuitions through the narrow channel of language — is what creates clarity. Not the other way around.

## Why this matters

If you're building something, making decisions, or leading a team, the quality of your thinking is the bottleneck. And the fastest way to improve your thinking is to write more — not for an audience, but for yourself.

A few things happen when you write regularly:

You discover what you actually believe, as opposed to what you think you believe. These are often different. The gap between them is where the interesting work lives.

You find the holes. A half-formed idea can survive in your head indefinitely. On paper, it has about thirty seconds before the cracks show.

You build a record. Six months from now, you'll want to know what you were thinking and why. Memory is unreliable. Writing is not.

## The practice

It doesn't need to be polished. It doesn't need to be public. It just needs to be regular.

A few hundred words a day. A decision log. A weekly reflection. The format matters less than the consistency.

---

The people who seem to think most clearly aren't smarter than everyone else. They've just spent more time writing things down.
